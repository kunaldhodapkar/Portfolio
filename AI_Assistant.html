Old age comes with a multitude of challenges. Coping with the effects of aging, physical weakness, and cognitive decline can make the simplest of tasks demanding for older adults.

To tackle this challenge, Emory Brain Health Center partnered with Georgia Tech to design new technologies that support independent living among older adults with cognitive decline.


My Role

I was the Lead Product Designer in a team of 5, focused on designing AI experiences for older adults (60+) and testing them out in their natural environment. Our team comprised a project manager, two UX researchers, and an engineer.

It was a 10 month long project from August 2020 – May 2021.

This skill was deployed to millions of users using Google Nest devices.

Chapter 1

Discovering the problem

We took a user-centered approach to this challenge. We started by understanding users' current context rather than enforcing the latest technologies. UX researchers conducted ethnographic research and interviews to understand their daily routines and points of struggle. They also piloted a study by placing voice AI assistants in couples' homes to know how they naturally interact with it. I helped with data analysis & coding.

Our users

Our users consisted of an elderly couple, a member (an elder struggling with cognitive decline), and their care partner.


Ethnographic research findings

In almost 60% of interactions with the conversational assistant, the couples tried to set alarms for medications. Not taking medicines on time can severely impact their long-term health. Globally, 75% of older adults struggle with medication management, sometimes missing critical doses and sometimes taking multiple doses.

From the research, we understood that managing medications was a big pain.

I saw an opportunity to build an AI experience to support them with their daily medications. I aligned with the leadership on this idea and formulated the "How Might We" question:

How might we use conversational AI to support millions of elderly adults in managing their daily medications?
We used Google Nest as our pilot conversational AI for it's ability to handle long-form prompts.
Chapter 2

Getting deeper insights

To understand how a new AI intervention can fit into couples' ecosystem, I collaborated with the UX researcher to conduct 2 focus groups with 50 couples. Goal was to understand their existing medication management strategies.

Important Note: This project was ongoing while the COVID-19 pandemic was at its peak. As older adults are not so adept with technology, we had to devise innovative ways to make virtual sessions engaging. Thus, instead of using a traditional Q&A format, we created an interactive quiz.


Systems Thinking

I also interviewed other teams involved in the program to understand an elderly couple's holistic environment.

Members' immediate care network often consisted of spouses and adult children (if local), and the secondary level consisted of a therapeutic team, nurses, and doctors.

The conversational AI was situated in members' immediate vicinity and formed a part of their primary care network.

Conversational AI was part of the primary care network

Research Insights

  • Current medication management systems were not robust: Couples often used pillboxes to keep track of their medications. They placed pillboxes strategically near the objects of their daily routine, like toothbrush holder or stove. Alarms were ineffective, because they lacked context. As a result, members missed medications often or sometimes overdosed.

  • Members thought of AI as a real person, followed advice: Members thought of conversational AI as a real person talking to them. Their mental model of this technology was very different from ours. Thus, we had a greater responsibility of designing a skill that's safe.

  • Spouses were the keepers: Members relied heavily on their care partners to check on them about their medications. But when both forgot, it caused the member to miss their dose.

Can conversational AI relieve the care partners from the responsibility of reminding members about their medications?
Chapter 3

Design goals

I formulated the design goals based on the research.

Fit seamlessly into existing routine

The AI intervention shouldn't compel the elderly couple to form new habits. It should fit seamlessly into their routine.

Mitigate the risk of double dosing

The AI system should prompt reflection rather than being a command or suggestion.

Accommodate diverse scenarios

Design should be reliable, contextually aware, and take into not just the highest probability scenarios but also variations.

Chapter 4

Mapping probable scenarios

To map different scenarios that couples encounter daily and understand their expectations around AI behavior, I partnered with the UX researcher. They suggested to conduct the participatory design activity with 35 couples, where we personified AI as "Rosie-the-robot" from Jetsons.

I storyboarded different scenarios and we asked users to help us write the story. What they would want "Rosie-the-robot" to do when such a scenario happens. It was a powerful way to understand user expectations around AI intervention.

Participatory design activity with elderly couples


Expectations From Conversational AI

  • Persist reminder if busy: Users mentioned they would want the system to persistently check in with them if they miss the reminder instead of just triggering it once.

  • Multi-platform design: Users also mentioned they would like to be reminded on their phone if they are not around at the time of medications.

  • Positive reinforcement on task completion: Users mentioned, they would be delighted if AI compliments them if they took the medications before the reminder went off.

Technical limitations that impacted design

Google Nest Hub is a protected hardware which limited our ability to customize the device. Some limitations that impacted design:

  • Integration with third-party pillboxes not supported: Ideally, we wanted to trigger the reminder only when a member forgets to take his pills, but since Google Nest doesn't support integrations with third-party pillboxes, it limited our ability to make the prompt contextual or store medication history.

  • Presence sensing: We wanted to customize the sound of the prompt based on whether someone was present in the room. However, we could not add this customization due to a lack of presence-sensing hardware in Google Nest.

  • Ability to snooze: Google restricted our ability to auto-invoke the medication alert based on response history. It had to be either statically invoked by time or explicitly by user speech. Thus, it restricted our ability to add a "snooze" feature to the design.

Deployment

Once I had built the skill for Google Nest Hub, I personalized it to match every couple's medication schedules and deployed it for 15 senior couples for testing.

Chapter 6

Final Designs


Designing for safety

Members' listened to the AI as it were a real person. Med Companion had to provide context without being overly suggestive as it'd risk double dosing. I carefully structured the prompt to avoid any direct suggestion, instead AI checked-in about the medications to encourage reflection.




Name personalization to establish trust

Med Companion was personalized for each member and addressed them by name. It served dual purpose, as there were two adults in the house, using names made it clear to the members that the reminder was for them, removing ambiguity. Second, it made them more responsive to the reminder.




Time personalization for a seamless experience

Med Companion was triggered at their medication times for each member. Time personalization allowed it to seamlessly fit into the existing schedules of the couples without being intrusive. Rather than forcing them to take the medications at a specific time, the skill triggered at their medication time.




Positive reinforcement to build sustainable habits

Med Companion employed the use of positive reinforcement to build sustainable habits. Conversational AI applauded when the users took before reminder, offered motivation if they forgot.




Designing for familiarity

As Med Companion was a multi-modal skill, the visual interface was designed with supporting visuals and familiar chime to make it easily recognizable.




Suggestion prompts for accessibility

I included suggestion prompts at each stage to make Med Companion accessible to those hard of hearing.

Chapter 7

Impact

After three weeks of deployment, we conducted semi-structured interviews and analyzed interaction logs to understand the skill performance. The feedback was positive about the conversational AI helping them remember to take their medications.



Qualitative Feedback

  • Applause received mixed feedback: Some users mentioned that they liked it when AI applauded them when they had taken the medications before the reminder went off; however, some mentioned that they found it patronizing. There was also a mention of the applause volume being too loud.

  • Conversational AI prevented them from missing medications: Users recalled instances where they had forgotten to take the medications, but because AI reminded them, they took it.



Quantitative Feedback

I analyzed interaction logs and found that in 20% of the cases, users had taken the medications after conversational AI had reminded them. Hence, the skill was effective in achieving its objective.

Missed medications had dropped by almost 20% after the skill deployment.


✨Outcome✨

This skill was shipped millions of users with Google Nest Hub devices, so they could customize their own AI assistant for medication management.

Chapter 8

Learnings from the experience


  • Imperfect solution is better than no solution: As we worked with closed hardware, we hit technical roadblocks at every step. We had designed a complete solution, but when it came to implementation, we were only able to implement half of it. However, that itself was a huge step forward from before for the couples, as managing medications was a real pain. Our solution could have been better, but it was still helpful. As a designer, there's a desire to ship a perfect solution; however, being mindful of the technical limitations and keeping at the forefront the wish to maximize value for our users can help to break down the perfect vision into achievable milestones.

  • Working with a challenging demographic: This project was my first experience working with the elderly population. It was challenging and rewarding in many respects. Older adults are not good with technology, and even the simplest of tasks, taking a remote survey or allowing remote access via Zoom, was a challenge. However, when we saw the positive impact of our work on their lives, the effort felt worthwhile as we were working with a vulnerable population that is not often focused on in society when it comes to emerging technologies.

  • Collaborating remotely: I planned my week to ensure I covered important topics during my 1:1s with stakeholders. Planning became increasingly important due to the lack of opportunities for small conversations. I also met weekly with the team to catch up on important updates, share progress, and discuss the next steps. I also organized "working sessions" with my teammates if we prepped for participatory design activity. I created a task tracker to align what everyone was working on and shared async updates. Remote collaboration was new, but I was always seeking ways to succeed.

  • Tremendous possibilities of multi-modal AI: Multi-modal AI was a relatively new technology in 2020. However, even at that point, I could realize the immense possibilities with it. I wrote an article sharing my views about the future of AI at that time, predicting that this technology has the potential to percolate almost every sphere of our lives.
